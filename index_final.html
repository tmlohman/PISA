Is the United States Really Failing Our Students?

If you have any personal investment in the world of United States public education, than your Facebook feed might look something like mine. The memes, shared over and over again, tell us that Common Core is making math unneccessarily difficult; that Japan is doing a much better job at teaching children social responsibility; that every other country in the world is providing better school lunches; that Finland is the model we should all aspire to. Perhaps you're wondering why the United States has fallen so far behind. Or, perhaps you're wondering IF the United States has really fallen so far beind. Let's have a look at some data and find out.


For this investigation, we will be using the 2012 PISA dataset. The PISA, or Programme for International Student Assessment, is a survey given to 15-year-old students around the world. It not only assesses their educational achievement in math, reading, and science, but also collects a wealth of information about their home and family life, their schools, their available technology, and so on. Since the PISA is conducted every three years, and extensive data analysis is performed before making the results public, the most current data available is from 2012.

What is the PISA?

The Programme for International Student Assessment (PISA) is a triennial international survey which aims to evaluate education systems worldwide by testing the skills and knowledge of 15-year-old students. To date, students representing more than 70 economies have participated in the assessment. This description was borrowed from oced.org.

What is ESCS?

ESCS is a composite metric of students' social, cultural, and economic status. It factors in such variables as parents' education level, language spoken at home, and number of books in the house. Due to cultural variatons, each country has a unique formula determined using PCA. The higher the value, the better off the student is, with an ESCS value of 0 being middle class. More information on ESCS is available in the PISA Technical Report, beginning on page 351.
